I actually started this blog in October 2024. Since then, I have yet to make a single post, besides updating my "about" and "now" pages every few months.

The short answer as to *why* is because I've been *scared*… 
- To put my own thoughts out in public
- Of exposing how unconfident I am about my own writing ability
- Of being perceived as dumb for saying something wrong or ineloquently
- Of making a bad impression on potential employers
- Of feeling disappointed for not keeping up yet another commitment I abandon

I've written blogs before, but I've never been consistent for more than a few weeks. This time, I feel like I'm starting a new chapter of my life (grad school), and I feel like it's time to develop my online presence. This probably won't be a totally professional blog... there's still a lot of things to figure out.

My anxiety comes from this dichotomy of wanting to be seen vs. scared of being seen. I'm very much a 'child of the internet', my childhood shaped by countless hours spent with online friends and posting across platforms like Tumblr, Twitter, DeviantArt, and Livestream. I still had the same fears of my artwork not being "good enough", my memes not being "funny enough" or not having enough "credibility" to back my opinions... but somehow, these thoughts didn't paralyze me the same way they do as an adult. Maybe I was rash. I definitely did make lots of mistakes during those times. But I was still in the habit of taking **action**. I want to tap into that.

After some thought, I've found that I like to have boundaries and control over how much of myself I'm allowing to be witnessed. I've fleshed out my idea of what the point of the internet is: **to put yourself out there and allow your people to find you.**

## Writing is Challenging

> "Challenge builds character"

The world seems to be increasingly moving toward shorter and shorter-form content. In protest, I want to be intentional about doing things that challenge my attention span--**reading more books, writing, revising, and writing some more**; watching old movies, sitting with my thoughts rather than giving into impulses to do *something* about them. I want to practice patience. I want to be more present. I want to take my time to do things, even if it makes me appear slow. **I want to stop bailing myself out of the hard things**. A good way to start is by writing frequently, building discipline. The only way to improve at writing is to keep doing it.

## Writing is a Form of Discovery
I want to develop my thoughts and build confidence in my opinions. *(YC techbro voice)* I want to write to think clearly. It's one thing to sit around, especially in my dark room, *thinking* about things on my mind. But for me, the intangibility of that makes it hard to see how that *thinking* is serving me. It's definitely not serving me when it turns into *overthinking*. 

Writing produces assets-- evidence of the work. I'm organizing my thoughts! I believe a lot in "learning generously", a core tenant I picked up during my time at the [Recurse Center](https://www.recurse.com/about). And my take on that is to "learn in public" through this blog--sharing things I'm learning, as I'm learning about them.

> "The action informs the action"

I have a lot of things I'm interested in: data, industrial design, dancing, art, comedy, yoga; the list goes on. I think that by fleshing out my thoughts on them by writing, it'll help me develop my understanding of who I am, what I believe in and value, and inform what to do next.

## Writing is a Way to Take Hold of Your Own Narrative

I'm coming to see that not every thought needs to be shared out loud (is this maturity? lol.) Often when I do share, I don't get the response I'm hoping for. But maybe what I'm really chasing is that *feeling* of being cared about, of mattering.

Mark Manson talks a lot about how our culture has become obsessed with *feeling* good-- feeling admired, included, validated. But chasing these feelings actually makes us more dependent on others' approval and thus fragile. He argues that breaking this "Feedback Loop from Hell" cycle requires reevaluating our values and rejecting societal norms that don't align with our authentic desires.

Joseph Nguyen, in "Don't Believe Everything You Think" says that 

> "Our feelings do not come from external events, but from our own thinking about the events. Therefore, we can only ever feel what we are thinking".

My understanding is that events are neutral, but our interpretation of them is what makes us feel some type of way... our thoughts determine our feelings. What I find compelling about this is that while we can't control what feelings arise, we *can* choose whether to engage with them. We can let those feelings pass through without taking them as signs from the universe, as things that define us.
### **TL;DR**

Writing becomes a way to engage with my thoughts without performing for an immediate reaction. Maybe it's off-putting, but I like having this one-sided space on the internet where I can say whatever I want.

> "If you're going to spend all of your time consuming content you may as well create some yourself"

## On Micro-Blogging
Seth Godin introduced me to the concept of "micro-blogging" after I read his "Small is the New Big", which is a collection of so many of his blog posts that he was able to form them into a small book. It made me realize that a lot of my other role models also, in some capacity, blog: Craig Newmark, [James Hoffmann](https://jimseventemp.wordpress.com/author/jimkingseven/), [Matt Webb](https://interconnected.org/), [Nicky Case](https://ncase.me/), [Julia Evans](https://jvns.ca/), [AnnaTheRed](https://annathered.wordpress.com/), Jonny Sun (kinda, if you would consider the snippets in his book to be blog-like... the point is that he writes a lot), as well as my many friends I met at Recurse Center (there's a big blogging culture there!) like Michael and Shenai. My good friends, Jenna, Mai and Ashley write too. I deeply admire them all.

One way I'm going to lower the stakes is by micro-blogging... writing as much or as little as I want, as long as it's frequent. This entire blog might end up as a series of "shitty drafts". 

As I said, there's still a lot to figure out, but what's changed is that I'm going to write *to* figure it out.
