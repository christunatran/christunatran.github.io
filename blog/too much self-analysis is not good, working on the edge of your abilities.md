this is more of a self-reminder. i may be self-analyzing right now, just as i have been for the past 2 posts and the past several weeks.

i have a habit of mistaking worry for a problem that needs immediate solving through analysis. my friend emily and i once jokingly vowed to stop trying to intellectualize our feelings, and just letting them pass instead of putting everything in paper. the overthinking often intensifies the pain and leads to rumination. as my mom calls it, "nghĩ bậy", or literally, "thinking about the wrong things". poison for the mind. instead, i should channel that energy into outlets that demand my full mental and physical focus for the moment.

*action*! 

*the only way out is through.*

this is why it's so important to find projects that are at the "edge of your abilities." 

as [recurse center](https://www.recurse.com/self-directives) puts it: 
> "Working at the edge of your abilities means being ambitious about what you can achieve and also honest about your capacity, choosing work that challenges you but isn't so far beyond your current abilities that it's discouraging. this is where the most learning and growth happens."

what gives me hope:
> "the edge of your abilities is dynamic, and changes over time. if you consistently push yourself to work at this edge, your abilities will expand, and what was once hard for you will become easier. to keep learning, you'll have to move on to more challenging work. these small gains compound over time, until you're able to do things that seemed impossible when you started."

and a sobering line:
> "the edge of your abilities also changes based on your circumstances each day... [sometimes] your capacity is reduced, and you have to adjust your expectations of yourself. it's also important to not go too easy on yourself. if you show up every day and work at the edge of your abilities for that day, you will grow."

### now
this week, i haphazardly embarked on 3D printing my Big Nose at the last minute before my program's Winter Show. many people discouraged me from taking on that stress, but i'd already committed. i do have a backup plan to present a prototype version of my piece, but i'm not so enthused about it to share and stand next to it for 3+ hours. even if this lofty endeavor leads nowhere, this push was what i needed to finally learn skills i've been wanting to learn *for years*. if not now, then when? 

*this* was my impetus to get to the edge of my abilities. i'm running back and forth across the engineering makerspace to get trained on various specialized printers, learning my options, painfully 3D modeling only to realize my model is broken, or discovering i chose the wrong software from the start. i've remade the model at least 5 times now, but i've definitely gotten better at fusion 360, learned meshmixer, and absorbed various esoteric knowledge about different slicer software and print preparation. i've become a regular customer of the makerspace, with its staff sighing whenever they see me approaching with yet another question. fighting the stress of only being able to do my print during limited hours. i keep making mistakes, but as long as i take the time to address them, i'm learning so much from them. there's so many new levels to 3D printing that i'm discovering, especially at the large scale. it's humbling and exciting.

i'm losing sleep over this. i joked that "the only thing that can heal me is if i rebuild this nose." of course i don't mean that literally, but that shows how much this matters to me. i've committed to this and i want to see how far i can get, [even if it leads nowhere](https://youtu.be/08DjMT-qR9g?si=krf9XpZODwvvisgb). i've already learned so much, and that's the outcome in itself.

what else would i be doing with this time? this is a lull in the semester when i'd typically take it easy... but as long as i have the capacity, and this awareness of how limited my time here is-- if not now, then when?

and a message to my friends: i'm okay, just let me cook right now.
